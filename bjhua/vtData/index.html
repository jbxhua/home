Vermont PCB Data

The overall goal of this project is to develop a web-based tool for exploratory analyses of the distribution of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) in school air throughout the state of Vermont and determine which communities are disproportionately affected. The target audience for this tool is primarily government officials and researchers. A major outcome of this site is to assist government officials in prioritizing schools and communities that need the most help with remediation using air concentration data and social vulnerability data.

Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) are a class of toxic chemicals that were used for a variety of industrial applications such as in heat transfer fluids, capacitors, caulking, and adhesives. PCBs are carcinogens, neurotoxins, and endocrine disruptors. Because of these toxic effects, PCBs were banned in the late 1970s. However, these chemicals are still present throughout the outdoor and indoor environment. In schools, PCBs were used in building materials such as caulking, sealants, and fireproof coatings. Children are especially vulnerable to the harmful effects of PCBs, therefore finding and removing PCB-containing materials in schools is crucial for preventing exposure.

Remediation of PCBs typically require complete removal of any and all PCB-containing materials. This is an expensive and time consuming process. Vermont is a very small, rural state and cannot fully fund complete remediation of every contaminated schools. Consequently, the burden is placed on the community to pay for corrective action. However, many communities are not able to fund complete remediation of their schools and require state or federal assistance. This tool aims to help government officials effectively allocate resources to affected communities based on the severity of contamination and metrics like social vulnerability. This tool will also help analyze the spatial distribution of PCB concentrations in school throughout the state of Vermont and attempt to identify trends that could help inform both communities and state officials.
